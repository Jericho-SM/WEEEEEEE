BEFORE THE BIG BANG

The universe began approximately 13.8 billion years ago from an extremely hot, dense singularity in an event known as the Big Bang. It has been expanding and cooling ever since, evolving from a particle soup into structured galaxies.

What is the big bang theory?

Despite its name, it wasn't a giant explosion in space, like a firecracker going off in an empty room. Instead, it was the explosive expansion of space itself. Imagine the entire universe—every galaxy, star, planet, and atom—compressed into a space smaller than a single atom. This point, called a singularity, was infinitely dense and intensely hot. About 13.8 billion years ago, this singularity began to rapidly expand and cool. As it cooled, the energy transformed into matter, eventually forming the building blocks of everything that exists.

In a fraction of a second, the universe underwent cosmic inflation, expanding faster than the speed of light from a subatomic speck into a massive expanse. Within minutes, it became a super-hot "primordial soup" of elementary particles, which took 380,000 years to cool enough for the first hydrogen and helium atoms to form, clearing the cosmic fog and allowing the "first light"—the Cosmic Microwave Background—to travel freely through space. After a dark period lasting 100 to 400 million years, gravity condensed massive gas clouds to ignite the first stars. Over the next several billion years, these stars grouped into galaxies and forged heavier elements in their cores, eventually providing the building blocks for planets and our own solar system, which formed about 4.6 billion years ago.

Related Theories

1. The Standard View: There Was No "Before"

According to Albert Einstein's theory of General Relativity, the Big Bang wasn't just the creation of matter and energy—it was the creation of space and time itself.

Because time is woven together with space into a fabric called "spacetime," time literally began at the exact moment of the Big Bang. Therefore, asking what happened before the Big Bang is like asking, "What is north of the North Pole?" Once you reach the North Pole, the concept of "north" stops making sense. In the same way, if time started at the Big Bang, the concept of "before" simply doesn't exist.

2. The "Big Bounce" Theory

Some scientists believe the universe didn't start from nothing, but rather from the collapsed remains of a previous universe. In this model, the universe goes through infinite cycles of expansion and contraction.

Imagine a universe that expands, eventually runs out of momentum, and then gravity pulls everything back together into a massive "Big Crunch." That crunch compresses everything back down to a singularity, which immediately rebounds in a new Big Bang. If this is true, our universe is just the latest in an endless chain of universes breathing in and out.

3. The Multiverse and Eternal Inflation

Another popular idea in modern physics is "eternal inflation." This theory suggests that "space" is actually a rapidly expanding, infinitely large void filled with energy.

Within this massive void, localized pockets occasionally slow their expansion and crystallize into distinct universes—like bubbles forming in a pot of boiling water. In this scenario, our Big Bang was just one bubble popping into existence within a much larger, eternally expanding "multiverse" that has always existed.

4. String Theory and Colliding Branes

In advanced physics like String Theory, our universe might be a 3D surface (called a "brane") floating in a higher-dimensional space. According to this highly theoretical idea, there are other branes floating out there as well. When two of these massive, universe-sized branes drift together and collide, the immense energy of the crash creates a Big Bang, spawning a new universe.
